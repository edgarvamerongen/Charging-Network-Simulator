About NRG2fly

NRG2fly is a Dutch company dedicated to the charging infrastructure that electric aviation needs to get off the ground. We help airports across Europe prepare for the first electric aircraft and the rotations that follow, working alongside aircraft manufacturers, grid partners, and regional stakeholders.

We support airports across three areas, from first idea to live operation:

Consulting

Electrification strategy, demand modelling, and the roadmap from a single charger to a full energy hub.

Hardware

Selecting and integrating the right chargers for aircraft, matched to your aircraft mix and grid connection.

CPO & MSP

Operating the charging points and the mobility-service layer, so charging is reliable from day one.

What this report means for your airport

This document is the output of our Charging Network Simulator. It translates a concrete flight schedule into the energy and power your airport would need to serve it: kWh per day, peak kW, the number of chargers, and how rotations fill the operating day. The figures are indicative — a strategic sizing aid rather than a procurement specification.

Its real value is as a starting point for a conversation. A single electric route already raises the questions every airport will face: where the peak demand sits, how it interacts with your existing grid connection, when a second charger becomes necessary, and how to avoid stranded assets as volumes grow.

Answering them turns charging from a cost line into the foundation of the airport as an energy hub: a site that not only charges aircraft, but balances peaks, integrates local generation and storage, and can serve ground vehicles and other users from the same infrastructure. Where electrification becomes a strategic advantage rather than a compliance exercise, we would be glad to take the next step: refine these assumptions against your real schedule, model the grid and its phasing, and build the roadmap from a first charger to a full energy hub.

Methodology & assumptions

Battery model

Per-leg energy is computed from the aircraft's stated battery (kWh) and range (km): leg_energy = battery × distance / range. By default every aircraft charges to a global charge target (set in Model settings, 80% by default), which individual airports can override — a local target always wins. With no target in effect the model falls back to deficit charging: a return trip recharges only the deficit that remains after the round trip (max(0, 2 × leg_energy − battery)), unless Recharge to full tops both ends up to 100% of capacity.

Multi-leg routing

When a single battery can't reach the destination the planner inserts charging stops along the way using a shortest-path (A*) algorithm that minimises total distance, with an optional bias toward a preferred airport size (e.g. medium-size airports). At each stop the aircraft charges to the applicable charge target — but never below what the next leg needs plus reserve; the terminal airport (one-way destination, or retour home) charges to that target too. Users can override any stop or replan.

Rotation scheduler

A rotation is one aircraft cycle: depart → charge at destination → return → recharge at home. The same aircraft can't fly two rotations at once, and each charger serves one aircraft at a time. When multiple flights compete for the same charger, the scheduler queues them and propagates the resulting wait through the rest of that aircraft's day.

Time horizon

The day runs 07:00 → 23:00 (16 hours of operation). The schedule loops nightly; any rotation that doesn't complete before 23:00 is flagged.

This report is indicative: the figures reflect the flight schedule and charger config the user entered in the Charging Network Simulator. Real-world energy use varies with payload, weather, wind, taxi time, reserves, charger efficiency, and grid losses — none of which are modelled here. Treat the numbers as a strategic sizing aid, not a procurement specification.
